Sell-Side Research · SWS Research

State-Owned Bank Investment Value Analysis

40-page comparative framework across China's six major state-owned banks evaluating asset quality cycles, NIM trajectory, expansion capacity, and dividend reliability.

Equity ResearchBanking SectorDuPont AnalysisWind

Background

This report was produced for an institutional client during my internship at SWS Research in mid-2024, at a time when China's state-owned banks were attracting renewed interest from insurance capital and index-tracking funds seeking high-dividend, low-volatility exposure.

Methodology

I constructed a five-dimensional evaluation framework covering profitability drivers (ROE decomposition, NIM trajectory), balance sheet expansion capacity, asset quality cycles, dividend reliability, and management quality. Data was sourced from Wind and company filings for all six banks across 2013–2024.

Key findings

  • Agricultural Bank of China (ABC) stood out on both expansion momentum and asset quality improvement — its NPL ratio fell 108bps from the 2016 peak to 1.08% by mid-2024, the steepest improvement among the six.
  • State-owned banks as a group offered a structurally lower NIM floor, but their liability cost advantage and earlier pricing adjustment meant NIM compression had more limited downside from 2024 onwards.
  • Bank of China presented the most nuanced risk profile: overseas asset exposure benefited from the global rate hike cycle, but potential Fed rate cuts and above-peer urban investment / property loan concentration warranted caution.
  • Dividend reliability was a key differentiating factor — state-owned banks averaged a 32% payout ratio in 2023, with regulatory guidance explicitly mandating a minimum 30% floor, making dividend income more predictable than for joint-stock peers.

Conclusion

ABC was the top pick: strongest loan growth, most improved asset quality, and a management team with clear strategic direction. ICBC and CCB offered stable fundamentals with sufficient infrastructure project pipelines to sustain credit growth in an asset-scarce environment.

Sell-Side Research · SWS Research

SME Bank Supply-Side Reform: Who Will Survive?

30-page industry report on consolidation dynamics in China's regional banking sector, with a four-dimensional evaluation framework and case studies on Changshu Bank vs Jinzhou Bank.

Industry ResearchCredit RiskRegulatory Analysis

Background

Written as part of SWS Research's "New Cycle, New Landscape" series on China's banking sector. The report addressed a structural question: as regulatory reform centralises financial supervision and forces consolidation among weaker regional banks, which institutions are positioned to survive — and which will be absorbed?

Methodology

I developed a four-dimensional evaluation framework assessing regional environment (fiscal health, economic structure, asset quality pressure), asset-side characteristics (loan growth, pricing power, concentration risk), liability stability (deposit base, funding cost rigidity), and profitability resilience. I applied this framework to two contrasting case studies — Changshu Rural Commercial Bank and Jinzhou Bank — to demonstrate how the same sector dynamics produce divergent outcomes.

Key findings

  • Regional economic fundamentals are the primary determinant of small bank viability: banks anchored in the Yangtze River Delta — with diversified industrial bases and strong fiscal self-sufficiency — structurally outperform peers in northeast and northwest regions where economic activity is concentrated in declining heavy industries.
  • Changshu Bank's decade-long focus on micro-enterprise lending in Suzhou demonstrated that niche client specialisation, consistently executed, can deliver above-peer loan growth (18.5% CAGR vs 13.2% sector average), below-1% NPL ratios, and a 117bps premium in loan yield — simultaneously.
  • Jinzhou Bank's failure path traced a clear pattern: early dependence on shadow banking assets to drive scale, followed by forced non-standard asset reclassification, a liquidity crisis triggered by over-reliance on interbank funding, and ultimately management misconduct that accelerated the collapse.
  • Banks with proprietary licences (e.g. Changshu's investment management bank licence) and established out-of-region branch networks are better positioned to participate actively in the consolidation wave rather than being passively absorbed.

Conclusion

Consolidation is inevitable, but outcome is not uniform. Banks with consistent strategy, quality regional anchoring, and sufficient capital buffers will gain market share. The key differentiator is not size, but strategic coherence sustained across credit cycles.

Credit Analysis · SWS Research

Qingdao Rural Commercial Bank: Asset Quality Diagnostic

Standalone credit quality assessment of QRCB. Full ROE decomposition identifying credit cost as the primary drag, with granular NPL analysis by loan category and forward-looking risk indicators.

Credit AnalysisROE DecompositionNPL Analysis

Background

A standalone credit quality assessment of Qingdao Rural Commercial Bank (QRCB), one of Shandong Province's largest listed rural commercial banks. The analysis was prompted by QRCB's persistently elevated credit costs and ROE underperformance relative to both provincial peers (Qilu Bank, Qingdao Bank) and the listed rural commercial bank average.

Methodology

I structured the analysis around a full ROE decomposition to isolate the specific drag on profitability, followed by a granular NPL breakdown by loan category, vintage, and borrower type. I also assessed forward-looking indicators — special mention loan ratio, overdue rate, and annualised NPL formation rate — to judge whether the asset quality trajectory was improving or deteriorating.

Key findings

  • Credit cost was the single largest ROE drag: at 1.04% in 2023 — 60bps above the rural commercial bank average — it directly suppressed ROAA to 0.57%, well below provincial peers Qilu Bank (0.72%) and Qingdao Bank (0.58%).
  • The NPL problem was concentrated in legacy corporate exposures: manufacturing (6.86% NPL rate) and wholesale/retail trade (3.08%) reflected the structural impact of Shandong's supply-side reform on over-leveraged heavy industry clients from 2015 onwards.
  • Forward indicators remained concerning — special mention loan ratio of 5.79% (second highest among listed banks) and a Q1 2024 annualised NPL formation rate of 2.03% (vs ~0.7% sector average) suggested the stock of at-risk assets had not been fully recognised.
  • Critically, the root cause was not purely regional — internal control failures, a management team assessed as "incompetent" in the bank's own 2022 performance review, and a controlling shareholder with heavily pledged equity compounded the underlying economic pressures.

Conclusion

QRCB's underperformance illustrates that regional economic stress and weak governance interact multiplicatively. Even as legacy property and manufacturing NPLs stabilise, rising SME operating loan risk and structural internal control issues make a near-term ROE recovery difficult without management-level intervention.
